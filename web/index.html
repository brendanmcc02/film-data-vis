MEAN DECADE RATING

>= 10 Films

This graph needs a description. Data visualisation can tell stories and patterns, but it can often be misleading if context is not known. Obviously I did my best to filter out the extreme cases by only featuring decades with >= 10 films - for example, 1930 only has 3 films, so it can be skewed. However, you can't manipulate data too much to get the results you want.

In fairness, I thought films before the 70s and 80s would be much higher. If there is a film from the 2020s that has let's say a 7.0 IMDB rating and 65 metascore, I (and many others I would argue) would be much less likely to watch that film if it was made in the 60s or 70s. We'll see this later with the cinema graphs, but I will sometimes watch modern films that I know are poor, but watch it anyway for social reasons or if people are going to watch it in the cinema. Another reason is survivorship bias - an old, average film is less likely going to be watched by modern audiences because it gets lost in time. So, most older films are watched because they stood the test of time, the fact that they are remembered to this day is a testament to their quality or cultural impact.

These are things I have thought before seeing this graph, so in all honesty I thought the older films would have higher ratings. I think this is also because there are quite a few films deemed as 'classics' or 'must watches', but then I watch them and am not impressed. I'd say that balances it out.

It's eye-catching as well to see 2020 being the lowest. I would say it's because I started watching more 'bad' films either in cinema or for social reasons, like I mentioned before.

MEAN RATING OF EACH RUNTIME INTERVAL

>= 5 Films

In concept, I thought this graph could yield eye-opening results, but not really in all honest. However, like we saw with the Villeneuve film graph, 'boring' graphs can still have their own insights. The takeaway from this is that there is no clear correlation between film runtime and my liking of them.

Now obviously I'm not ignorant to the clear lead of <3h and <3h30m, but I would argue that this is mainly for one reason: if a film has a very long runtime, and the reviews are average, I am much less likely to watch the longer film, as opposed to a film with let's say a normal 2hr runtime. This creates a bias for the longer films, hence the higher mean rating.

MEAN RATING OF ENGLISH & INTERNATIONAL FILMS

Following the Academy Award definition of an International Film: The Film is produced outside the USA and has a >50% non-English dialogue track. So, I check if a film is produced outside the US (and the UK because realistically if a film is produced in the UK it will obviously have English be primarily spoken), and then I check if the first language in the film's language list is English. Unfortunately it's not perfect because, for example, Roma and All Quiet on the Western Front are international films, but because they are Netflix films, USA is included in its list of countries making it a non-international film.

I expected International Films to have a higher mean, mostly because (similar reasoning as before) I am less likely to watch an average international film as opposed to an average American film. I think that is just a result of human nature: we tend towards the familiar.

I am quite surprised by this result. I guess the takeaway here is that there is no clear correlation between my liking of a film, and whether it is international or not.

MEAN RATING OF FILMS WATCHED IN & OUT OF CINEMA

Okay this was very surprising. Before seeing this graph, rationally I expected the ratings of films watched in cinema to be higher - frankly because I am paying money for a ticket. Seeing a film at home is also much more convenient than going out to a cinema, and from memory there have been times when I have knowingly watched a film at home knowing the reviews were average, so I thought that would have brought the mean rating down.

Surprise, surprise. The opposite is true. My rationale for this is that sometimes I will watch a film in cinema for social reasons, not because I think the film is good but just because others are going to see it. This would bring the rating down. Furthermore, sometimes I will be anticipating watching a film in cinema, for example from a director I like, so I will go in blind. The film may be a flop or poor, for example Babylon (dir. Damien Chazelle) or Napolean (dir. Ridley Scott). Still a surprising result.

MY RATING DISTRIBUTION

My standard for 10/10 films is very high. The best way to describe it is that I give it to films that I have experienced a special awe after watching them.

It's natural to see a normal distribution emerging here - more interestingly however is that the vast majority of films are 6-8. There is a relatively big step from 5 to 6, and from 8 to 9. I would say this is because when rating films I can often be on the conservative side, for both good and bad films.

Obviously there is going to be a skew towards the higher-rated films, because generally I make an effort to watch films that are well-rated, or that I think I will enjoy.
